# The Technology and Society Strand
## Science & Technology · KMK Themenfeld 8 · DSP Prague · Grades 11–12

---

## What this strand is

Three instruments examining technological disruption at three different scales.

**Who Pays for Progress?** — Political. Who bears the cost of disruption, and what does society owe the people who pay it?

**Dude, Where's My Hoverboard?** — Historical and analytical. How do we recognise disruption when it is happening? Why does it so rarely look like what we expected?

**What Are You For?** — Existential. What happens when disruption reaches human capability itself? What remains worth becoming?

Together the instruments examine the same phenomenon from three angles:

- Distribution — who gains, who pays, and who decides
- Interpretation — how to read a technological change that is still in progress
- Meaning — what human purpose looks like when the work is automated

No single instrument covers the full territory. All three are needed for the strand to cohere.

---

## What the instruments are — and what they are not

Each instrument in this strand is designed as an encounter with a structured set of real cases. The word *instrument* is deliberate.

**Not a lesson.** A lesson delivers content and checks whether students have received it. These instruments do not deliver content. They present documented cases and ask students to think about them. The teacher does not have the correct interpretation in advance.

**Not a simulation.** A simulation asks students to inhabit a role. These instruments ask students to be themselves — to bring their own values, prior beliefs, and political instincts to cases that will complicate those instincts.

**Not a debate.** A debate assigns positions. These instruments ask students to discover and defend positions they actually hold, which is harder and more productive than defending an assigned one.

**Not a survey.** The response options are not designed to measure pre-existing opinion. They are designed to make students articulate a position precisely enough that they can see what it implies — and what it costs.

An instrument works when students leave with a more complicated version of the position they arrived with. It fails when students leave with the position they arrived with, unchanged. It also fails when students leave with no position at all — relativism is not the goal.

## The methodology

All three instruments share a common design architecture. Understanding the architecture matters because the architecture is what makes them work — and because the next instruments in the strand should follow it.

### 1. Cases before framework

Students encounter specific, documented, real cases before any analytical framework is named. The printing press before the attention/disruption table. The Luddites before the pattern. The Clarkesworld submissions before the argument about authorship.

This is a deliberate inversion of conventional teaching. The conventional approach introduces the framework and then applies it to examples. This approach gives students the examples and asks them to build the framework themselves. The framework that emerges from encounter is more durable than the framework that arrives as instruction.

The risk of naming the framework first: students apply it mechanically rather than understanding it. They learn to identify the Playbook rather than to see what the Playbook is seeing. They learn to call something co-optation rather than to understand what co-optation does. Framework-first teaching produces pattern-recognition without insight.

### 2. Gain and loss simultaneously

Every case in every instrument presents gain and loss as simultaneous consequences of the same change — not as opposites to be weighed against each other, but as the same event seen from different positions.

The printing press did not produce mass literacy *despite* producing religious war. It produced both from the same change. Containerisation did not benefit global trade *at the cost of* dockworker communities. The same technology produced both outcomes. The AI essay that receives an A does not help the student learn *instead of* helping them produce. It does both, simultaneously, from the same interaction.

This is the most important design principle in the strand, and the one most easily abandoned under pressure. When students want to resolve the tension — *so on balance, was it good or bad?* — the instrument should hold the tension open. The answer to that question is always *both*, and the interesting question is always *for whom*.

### 3. Gained by whom / lost by whom

The question in *Dude, Where's My Hoverboard?* is: what was gained, what was lost? The question in *Who Pays for Progress?* is one level sharper: gained by whom, lost by whom?

This shift is not a detail. It is the difference between a historical observation and a political argument. Students who can identify a gain and a loss are doing historical analysis. Students who can identify who specifically gained and who specifically paid — and recognise that these are often different people — are doing political analysis.

The strand moves from the first kind of thinking to the second. *Hoverboard* teaches the historical pattern. *Progress* politicises it. *What Are You For?* personalises it.

### 4. What the instruments refuse to do

The instruments are defined as much by what they refuse as by what they do.

**They refuse the binary.** Technology good / technology bad. Capitalism good / capitalism bad. AI good / AI bad. Progress good / progress bad. Every binary in the strand is dissolved by the case structure before students have a chance to settle into it. Students who arrive with the binary leave holding a more complicated version.

**They refuse the verdict.** None of the instruments culminates in a correct answer. The pattern table in *Hoverboard* does not tell students what to think about AI. The gain/loss table in *Progress* does not tell students whether UBI is the right policy. The closing question in *What Are You For?* — what remains worth becoming? — is not answered by the instrument.

**They refuse premature synthesis.** A conventional lesson moves from example to principle to application. These instruments resist the movement to principle. When the pattern becomes visible — when students discover that disruption is never where the attention is, or that the gains go to capital and the costs to labour, or that the lazy AI essay has no scar tissue — the instrument does not pause to celebrate the discovery. It immediately complicates it. The Farahany case in *Hoverboard* arrives precisely when students think they have understood the pattern. *Progress* ends with an empty row in the pattern table. *What Are You For?* ends on a question that has no answer the instrument can generate.

**They refuse to tell students how to feel.** The cases are presented with care — real events, real consequences, real people — but without editorial direction about the appropriate emotional response. Students who feel indifferent to the Luddites and students who feel outraged by them are both in the right instrument. The instrument does not adjudicate between those responses. It asks both to think more precisely about what their response implies.

### 5. Success is not consensus

A lesson that produces consensus has probably resolved the tension prematurely. These instruments are designed to increase productive disagreement — not to manufacture conflict, but to surface genuine disagreements that are already present and that would otherwise remain implicit.

The test of a successful session is not whether students agree at the end. It is whether the disagreements at the end are more precise, more substantive, and more honest than the disagreements at the beginning. Students who arrive saying "AI is bad" and leave saying "AI is bad for workers in cognitive professions who lack political representation, and I'm not sure what should be done about that" have had a successful encounter with the instrument.

Students who arrive saying "AI is bad" and leave saying "AI is bad" — unchanged, unexamined — have not.

## The strand sequence

The instruments can be used independently, but they are designed to work in sequence. The sequence matters.

**Start with *Who Pays for Progress?*** — it establishes the political register and the core question. Students learn to see disruption as a political event with specific beneficiaries and specific costs. They develop a habit of asking *gained by whom, lost by whom* rather than *gained or lost*.

**Then *Dude, Where's My Hoverboard?*** — it historicises the pattern. Students who have already asked *who pays?* about specific cases are better equipped to see the attention/disruption table as an analytical tool rather than a historical curiosity. They arrive at *Hoverboard* knowing the political stakes; the historical cases become more legible as a result.

**Then *What Are You For?*** — it personalises the question. Students who have established the political and historical patterns are now asked what those patterns mean for their own lives, purposes, and futures. The closing question — *what remains worth becoming?* — lands differently after the previous two instruments than it would in isolation.

**The transition between Progress and Hoverboard** — the moment to name the threshold explicitly. All six cases in *Progress* share a hidden assumption: there was always new work on the other side. The Luddite weavers became factory workers. The dockworkers' children found other employment. The assumption that this adjustment mechanism holds is exactly what the third instrument interrogates. This transition does not need a fourth instrument. It needs ten minutes in the classroom where the teacher names what the students have been circling.

## The strand in the context of Themenfeld 8

KMK Themenfeld 8 covers:

1. Scientific and technological progress and its impact on society
2. Visions of the future: utopia vs. dystopia

The three instruments in this strand cover the first requirement directly and the second obliquely. *Hoverboard* is the historical foundation — technology as a recurring pattern of disruption. *Progress* is the political analysis — who bears the cost of that disruption. *What Are You For?* is the present-tense encounter — what the disruption means for students now, in 2026, in their own lives.

The utopia/dystopia framing of the second requirement is deliberately not adopted in any of the three instruments. The reason is methodological. Utopia and dystopia are not opposites. They are the same change seen from different positions. The printing press was utopian for readers and dystopian for the institutional Church. Containerisation was utopian for global trade and dystopian for port communities. Every technology in *Hoverboard* and every case in *Progress* demonstrates this simultaneously. Building a separate instrument to establish that utopia and dystopia are not opposites would be redundant — the strand has already built that understanding through encounter.

The instruments that extend the strand into speculative futures — **Orientation 2089** and **ARIA** — take students from the analytical habits built in the first three instruments into genuine encounters with possible futures. Students who arrive at Orientation 2089 after the trilogy have been trained to ask: where is the attention, where is the disruption, who gains, who pays, what remains worth becoming? The 27 situations in Orientation 2089 are designed to be read with exactly those questions in hand.

## A note on the design decisions that are hardest to maintain

These instruments were built through an iterative process that repeatedly refined the design toward productive discomfort and away from premature resolution. The most important design decisions were also the ones most frequently pulled against.

**Against easy answers.** Every draft of every instrument contained moments where the argument became a conclusion — where the instrument told students what to think rather than presenting the conditions for thinking. These moments were consistently removed. The instruments are harder to use than a conventional lesson precisely because they do not tell students what to think. That difficulty is the point.

**Against binary framing.** The most common pull in the design process was toward *was this good or bad?* Every instrument resisted that pull by building the gain and the loss from the same change. This resistance was not ideological neutrality — the strand has a clear methodological commitment to complexity — but it is not the same as having no position. The position is: these questions are harder than the binary suggests, and students deserve to engage with the actual difficulty.

**Against the satisfying arc.** Every instrument ends in open water. The pattern table in *Hoverboard* has an AI row with a question mark. The six closing positions in *Progress* each carry an implicit cost that the instrument names but does not resolve. The closing question of *What Are You For?* is not answered. This was a deliberate choice made against the conventional desire for closure. The questions these instruments raise are not questions that close. Students who leave with the question intact, but held more precisely, have learned something more durable than students who leave with an answer.

The strand is not complete. The following instruments are planned or in development:

| Instrument | Status | Function in strand |

| Who Pays for Progress? | Complete | Political foundation |
| Dude, Where's My Hoverboard? | Complete | Historical/analytical layer |
| What Are You For? | Complete | Existential layer |
| Orientation 2089 | Complete | Future encounter — values under speculative pressure |
| ARIA | Complete | Future evaluation — judgment under consequence |
| What's Still Private? | Planned | Data, surveillance, and the limits of visibility |

*What's Still Private?* would extend the Farahany case in *Hoverboard* into a full instrument — examining what becomes data across successive technologies and asking whether there should be limits. Cases would include CCTV, social media tracking, facial recognition, and brain-computer interfaces. The closing question: is there anything about a person that should never become data?
