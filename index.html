Technology

Every technology arrives carrying a promise. Every technology changes something else as well. This strand asks what technological change actually does — to knowledge, to labour, to authorship, to the gap between what is known and what is believed.

What the Instruments Share
Design principle 1
Cases before framework
Students encounter specific, documented, real cases before any analytical framework is named. The framework that emerges from encounter is more durable than the framework that arrives as instruction.
Design principle 2
Gain and loss simultaneously
The gain and loss in every case come from the same change — not as opposites, but as the same event seen from different positions. The instrument holds both without resolving them.
Design principle 3
Gained by whom / lost by whom
The question is not whether progress was beneficial in aggregate. It is who specifically gained and who specifically paid — and whether those are the same people.
Design principle 4
Success is not consensus
Students who leave with more precise, more honest, more complicated versions of the positions they arrived with have had a successful encounter. Students who leave unchanged have not.
Where the framework fails
What the framework misses
The strand's emphasis on structural forces — who gains, who pays, what patterns repeat — can underemphasise contingency. Technological change does not follow a script. The printing press did not inevitably produce the Reformation. The internet did not inevitably produce what it produced. Ask at each instrument: what would the framework miss here?

Utopia and dystopia are not treated as opposites. Every case in the strand demonstrates that the same technological change can be utopian and dystopian simultaneously, depending on what you value and where you stand.

Threads across the instruments
Thread 1
The same change, different positions
  • Who Pays for Progress? and Dude, Where's My Hoverboard? both examine the same technological event — but one asks who bears the cost, the other asks where the disruption actually lands. Together they show that scale and distribution are different questions.
  • Why Doesn't Everyone Agree? asks why the evidence doesn't settle the argument. What Are You For? asks what remains when capability is no longer a differentiator. Both are about the gap between what is known and what is valued.
Thread 2
The pattern that keeps reappearing
  • Across all four instruments, the disruption follows the same pattern: a technology arrives promising one thing, produces something else as well, and the second thing is where the real change happens. The printing press was not about books. Containerisation was not about shipping. AI is not about intelligence.
  • The strand's recurring question: who noticed, who named it, and who had the power to shape what came next?
Abitur essay questions — Themenfeld 8

To what extent does technological progress benefit society as a whole — or does the question itself obscure what matters?

Why does scientific consensus fail to produce social consensus? What does the gap between knowing and believing tell us about how societies make decisions?

Is it possible to assess a technology's impact before that impact has fully arrived? What are the limits of historical analogy?

What is the difference between a skill made redundant by technology and a human capability diminished by it? Does the distinction matter?

To what extent is manufactured doubt a political problem rather than an epistemological one?

Should societies compensate those who lose from technological disruption? On what grounds — and at what scale?
